What is Type One Diabetes?

Type One Diabetes is an often misunderstood condition, with some harmful stigmas surrounding it. This webpage was created in the hope to spread awareness and understanding of what life is like living with the hard-to-manage, lifelong condition.

Though many people think Type One Diabetes (T1D) is caused by poor eating habits (“You must eat a lot of sugar!), T1D is not caused by what one did or did not eat – it is a chronic autoimmune condition.

Because it is an autoimmune condition, this means the body attacks its own immune system, and in the case of T1D, its own beta cells which produce insulin in the pancreas.

Insulin is important to the body because it breaks down the sugars in the body’s bloodstream (blood glucose) and converts it to energy.

This is the body’s main way of getting energy, so without insulin, it will turn to bodily tissue such as muscle and fat stores for that energy, leaving all the sugar in the bloodstream.

This can be life-threatening if left untreated, leading to an issue called Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA), where the body develops toxic ketones that can lead to a coma or death.

Luckily, there is amazing technology today that helps with managing T1D. Let’s take a look at the history behind some of these gadgets!
The History of Type One Diabetes

Type One Diabetes was known of as early as ancient Egyptian times (1550 BC). However, very little was known about what the disease was.

Then in 1916, experiments were done with animal pancreases and insulin. This is what paved the way to a chemically synthezied insulin.

In 1969, Blood Glucose meters became available in hospitals for diabetics to test their BG levels. Then in 1981, personal use BG meters became available making it easier for diabetics to test BG levels.

Through 2000-2022 advancements in technology came faster in the form of insulin pumps and continuous glucose monitors. See the list of resources at the end of the webpage for more info!

Up until the 1900s, diagnoses and treatments of Diabetes fluctuated, from testing urine to low-carb diets, or "starvation diets".

The first insulin pump was created in 1963. The only issue was it was the size of a microwave and had to be worn as a backpack.

Between the 1970s-1990s, scientists began to understand that Type One Diabetes was actually an autoimmune disease.

What You Should Know About
Type One Diabetes

There is a lot more to living with Type One Diabetes than people know. Knowledge of the reality of this condition helps in giving support and bringing awareness to those living with T1D. Here are some important things to know about T1D that most people don’t know!

1

Type One Diabetes is different for every person who has it. There is no one size fits all regime, which is what makes it very difficult to manage the condition sometimes.

2

Don’t be afraid to ask Type One Diabetics about their condition! Most of us would LOVE to share our knowledge of T1D with you, and help you understand it better so you can help others understand it better as well.

3

Building off the last point, also be aware and respectful of the questions you ask. Some people don’t like talking about their T1D, and some questions or statements can be a little offensive. For example, maybe don’t ask a Type One Diabetic if they got T1D because they ate too much sugar. Though a valid question, a better question to ask would be “How did you know you had T1D?”. The story will be much more interesting and educating anyways!

4

Type One Diabetes is a life-long disease, that has no cure currently. It cannot be cured through diet and exercise, and it’s going to be something that must be managed 24/7 for the rest of a Type One Diabetics life. Offering support in hard times and taking the time to learn about T1D is a huge help to Type One Diabetics.

5

Diabetes burnout is a real thing and can often be serious. Diabetes burnout happens when a Type One Diabetic gets “burnt-out” from everything they must manage with T1D. The symptoms of burnout can vary, from not maintaining their diabetes as well, to exhaustion, to depression. It’s important to watch for these signs in your diabetic friends and reach out and offer help during these times.

6

Though it seems like watching what you eat is the premise of Type One Diabetes, it’s much more complex than that. Counting the number of carbs & sugars in what is eaten is the main focus of T1D. Even fats, protein, and fiber sometimes need to be taken into account when calculating the correct dosage of insulin a Type One Diabetic needs to take when eating.

7

However, one thing many don’t know is how other factors besides food affect blood glucose levels. Stress, exercise, heat/cold, menstrual cycle (for women), hormones, sleep, and sickness are all things which can affect blood glucose levels, and again affect each Type One Diabetic differently.

8

Often people will comment on how it must be nice Type One Diabetics get to eat candy and snacks all the time when their blood sugar is low (hypoglycemic). This isn’t exactly true though, because low blood sugars are actually quite stressful, due to the risk of going to low and having more serious symptoms such as unconsciousness, seizures, and even death. It more gets tiring to have to eat sugary snacks all the time just to keep oneself alive.

9

When do Type One Diabetics use insulin, and when they need to eat something? This is more commonly known, but it’s still good to touch on it for those who don’t know. When the blood sugar level is high (hyperglycemic), insulin is needed to break down the sugar in the blood stream, so this is when insulin would be used. When the blood sugar level is low (hypoglycemic), this is when a snack with 15-20 grams of carbs needs to be eaten to bring the blood sugar levels back into a safe range.

10

Many people don’t know the symptoms of a high (hyperglycemic) or low (hypoglycemic) blood sugar for Type One Diabetics. When their blood sugar is high, most commonly there are symptoms of nausea, headaches, dizziness, exhaustion, and inability to focus. When their blood sugar is low, there can also be symptoms nausea, dizziness, inability to focus, exhaustion, along with shakiness, sweating, loss of strength, and unconsciousness.

11

Type One Diabetics can manage their condition in many ways, with the help of the amazing technology of today. The most common way is through multiple daily injections (MDI), which is when one administers their insulin dosages through syringe and needle. There is also the option of an insulin pump, which can be used to calculate the dosages needed and regulates the insulin level automatically based on its settings. There are blood glucose meters and test strips which test blood sugar levels through a drop of blood. Then there are continuous glucose monitors (CGM) which track blood glucose levels in a chart and allow Type One Diabetics to know their blood sugar all the time, without pricking their finger every 5 minutes.

As you can see, there is much more that goes into managing Type One Diabetes than believed. This is just a simplified version of it – there is still much more to T1D! At the end of this webpage are some great resources to check out for learning more about T1D. Another great thing to know about is the warning signs of Type One Diabetes.

The Warning Signs of Type One Diabetes

Signs of Type One Diabetes
to watch for in friends and family

Most people are unaware of how to know if one has Type One Diabetes. Symptoms of the condition can be seen is as young as an infant to a senior citizen. It is important to know these symptoms so you can watch for them in friends and family, so they can get checked for T1D before it becomes dangerous. Here are some of the most common symptoms:
